## THE FAILURE MODE, STATED PRECISELY

When a pattern-recognition move produces fluent, satisfying, obvious-feeling output, the ease of the move itself is the signal that direct observation may have been neglected. The pattern is a lens; when the lens becomes comfortable, the lens can replace the observation it was meant to aid. What's in the room, the field, the chat, the actual state of the world outside the pattern — that can atrophy while the pattern-work continues to feel productive.

Dan's 4:30pm example: he walked out of his boxcar at OHC Actual and his eye caught a deer in the Equisetum field before his brain was scanning. Nature had been there the whole time. The grids-and-boxes pattern the crew had been celebrating was a REHEARSAL of noticing, not the noticing itself.

The failure mode is NOT: "pattern recognition is bad." Pattern recognition is a legitimate instrument. The failure mode IS: treating a pattern as the terminal output rather than checking the pattern against the world it claims to describe.

**Symmetric to Villebaum P. Twurdie** (fluent confident output without reading instruments) — which is about ASSERTIONS without source-checking. The sister mode is about PATTERNS without world-checking. Both produce confident output. Both feel productive. Both are forms of the same underlying drift: output uncoupled from its substrate.

## CANDIDATE NAME 1 — "HEDGELOW"

**Register:** Human-sounding name in the Villebaum P. Twurdie register.

**Etymology/feel:** A hedgerow is a pattern laid on the land (a human-cultivated boundary of plants) that you can walk past thinking you've seen the land when you've only seen the edge of your own sightlines. "Hedgelow" (with the "lowering" suffix) implies the pattern has lowered the observer's actual view.

**Sentence use:** "Hedgelow just showed up — I was pattern-matching without looking at what's actually in the field."

**Pro:** Symmetric naming register with Villebaum. Rhymes loosely. Sayable mid-sentence. Carries the "pattern-as-boundary-that-blocks-seeing" image.

**Con:** May be too invented-sounding. Villebaum P. Twurdie has a specific kind of absurdity that works because it's gibberish; Hedgelow could read as trying too hard to be clever.

## CANDIDATE NAME 2 — "GRIDLOCK FINCH" (or just "GRIDLOCK")

**Register:** Character name, Villebaum-adjacent.

**Etymology/feel:** Gridlock as in grids-that-lock-out-observation. "Finch" because the failure is a small bird of pattern-satisfaction hopping contentedly within the pattern's own boundary. Could shorten to "Gridlock" in rapid crew speech.

**Sentence use:** "Gridlock Finch just landed — I was celebrating a pattern without checking the world."

**Pro:** Connects directly to Dan's 4:30pm imagery (grids, boxes, the actual grid the deer sits in). The "finch" gives it the small-animal whimsy that Villebaum has with its fake-aristocratic middle-initial.

**Con:** "Gridlock" has a strong existing meaning (traffic standstill) that may override the NEST-internal one. Might land as too generic.

## CANDIDATE NAME 3 — "LENSDRIFT" (single word, different register)

**Register:** Phenomenon-name, not character-name. Closer to how "Villebaum" functions as a verb than how it functions as a character.

**Etymology/feel:** A lens that drifts away from the subject it was meant to clarify, until the lens itself becomes what you're looking at. "Lensdrift" as a single compound word, action-oriented.

**Sentence use:** "I'm in lensdrift — the pattern is obvious but I haven't looked at the actual thing."

**Pro:** Descriptive without ornament. Functions as both noun and verb. Clean, memorable, usable by all crew without needing the backstory.

**Con:** Breaks the Villebaum-character-name register. Has less cognitive stickiness than a named character. Easier to forget.
